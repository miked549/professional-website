How to Roll Over a 401(k) to an IRA: 10 Things to Consider

Two common terms you’ll hear when planning your retirement are 401(k) and IRA. A 401(k) is an employer-sponsored account that allows you to set aside a portion of your earnings for retirement. Many companies match part of your contributions as an employment benefit, which is a strong incentive to participate. On the other hand, an individual retirement account (IRA) is a personal savings plan.

Moving money from a 401(k) to an IRA is called a rollover. This article will discuss the pros and cons of an IRA versus a 401(k) and how a 401(k) rollover to an IRA might benefit you.

10 Pros and Cons of Rolling Over a 401(k) into an IRA

Like any investment strategy, moving money between accounts has benefits and drawbacks.

Pros of Rolling a 401(k) into an IRA

If you’re wondering what the advantages are of rolling over a 401(k) to an IRA, here are a few things to keep in mind.

1. More Investment Choices

Employers and their third-party 401(k) providers choose which products you can invest in, often limiting you to a list of preapproved mutual and exchange-traded funds. With an IRA, you can pick from thousands of investment options, including stocks, bonds, and annuities, and rebalance your portfolio whenever you’d like.

2. Easier to Get Up-to-Date Information About Changes

When you leave a job, your employer may not prioritize updating you on 401(k) plan changes. With an IRA, you control the funds directly and can monitor your investments anytime.

3. Lower Fees and Costs

401(k) plans may include administrative, investment, and service fees. IRAs may also have fees, but you choose the provider and investments, giving you the option to shop for lower costs.

4. Possible Incentives Such as Cash or Free Stock Trades

Some financial institutions offer bonuses or free stocks for rollovers. While not the main reason to switch, it can add value.

5. Fewer Rules and Barriers to Entry

Employers can set their own 401(k) rules, which vary by company. IRAs generally have fewer restrictions, offering more flexibility.

Cons of Rolling a 401(k) into an IRA

1. Greater Buying Power with a 401(k)

401(k) plans often have access to institutional pricing and employer matching, which can make them more cost-effective.

2. 401(k) Tax Savings

Working past retirement age? A 401(k) may let you delay required minimum distributions (RMDs) if you stay with your employer, avoiding taxes until withdrawal.

3. 401(k)s Have Better Legal Protection

ERISA laws protect most 401(k) assets from bankruptcy and lawsuits, whereas IRA protections vary by state.

4. Early Retirement Benefits (Rule of 55)

Withdraw from a 401(k) penalty-free starting at 55 if you leave your job; IRAs require you to wait until 59½ to avoid early withdrawal penalties.

5. 401(k) Stable Value Funds

Some 401(k)s offer stable, low-risk funds not available in IRAs.

How to Rollover a 401(k) into an IRA: 2 Methods

1. Direct Rollover

Your 401(k) provider sends funds directly to your IRA. Taxes depend on account types:

  • Traditional 401(k) to Traditional IRA: No taxes during transfer.
  • Roth 401(k) to Roth IRA: No taxes, both use after-tax dollars.
  • Traditional 401(k) to Roth IRA: Taxes owed since one is pre-tax and the other is after-tax.

2. Indirect Rollover

Your 401(k) provider sends funds to your bank account. You have 60 days to deposit them into an IRA to avoid taxes and penalties. 20% is withheld for taxes, and you must replace it to roll over the full amount.

Should You Roll Your 401(k) into an IRA?

Consider a rollover if you have 401(k) funds with a former employer, want more investment options, or want to consolidate accounts. You may prefer keeping your 401(k) if you want higher contribution limits, institutional pricing, early retirement benefits, or employer matching.

FAQ

What’s a Rollover IRA?

A rollover IRA lets you deposit funds from a company-sponsored account into a personal plan.

Is a 401(k) an IRA?

No. A 401(k) is employer-sponsored; an IRA is an individual account.

If I Leave My Job, Do I Have to Roll My 401(k) into an IRA?

No. You can leave funds where they are, roll them into a new employer’s 401(k), or cash out (taxes and penalties may apply).
